## 🧠 Note


Since your platform is optimized for **plasma membrane proteins**, we can strip away the intracellular and mitochondrial targets and zero in on those that actually reside at or near the cell surface in Schwann cells or axons.

Let’s look at the major CMT genes through that lens:

---

### **1. PMP22 (Peripheral Myelin Protein 22)**

**Localization:** Tetraspan membrane protein in the **Schwann cell plasma membrane**.  
**Mechanistic role:** Regulates myelin compaction and Schwann cell differentiation.  
**Pathogenic mechanism (CMT1A):** PMP22 overexpression (via gene duplication) causes misfolded protein accumulation and myelin instability.  
**Suitability for mini-binders:** Excellent.  
You could design binders that:

- Modulate PMP22 trafficking (stabilize properly folded forms or redirect misfolded ones for degradation).
- Alter cell-surface stoichiometry (competitive binding could modulate PMP22-PMP22 or PMP22-integrin interactions).
- Potentially serve as extracellular “correctors” if they bind the exposed loops of PMP22 on Schwann cell membranes.

This is one of the **few CMT targets squarely in the plasma membrane**—very well aligned with your platform’s design space.

---

### **2. GJB1 / Connexin 32 (CMTX1 subtype)**

**Localization:** Gap junction channel protein—**plasma membrane-resident** in Schwann cells, forming connexons between paranodal loops.  
**Mechanistic role:** Mediates ion and metabolite transfer between layers of the myelin sheath.  
**Pathogenic mechanism:** Loss or dysfunction of gap junction communication; many missense variants cause trafficking defects or channel instability.  
**Suitability:** Strong potential for plasma-membrane binding.  
Mini-binders could:

- Stabilize connexon assembly or intercellular docking.
- Selectively bind mutant forms to restore trafficking or prevent degradation.
- Possibly “bridge” channel interfaces if properly engineered.

This is another **membrane-proximal and accessible** target—arguably even more druggable than PMP22 because the extracellular loops of connexins are well characterized structurally.

---

### **3. MPZ (Myelin Protein Zero)**

**Localization:** Transmembrane glycoprotein; **extracellular adhesive domain** critical for myelin compaction.  
**Mechanistic role:** Homophilic adhesion in Schwann cell membranes.  
**Pathogenic mechanism (CMT1B):** Mutations cause adhesion loss or misfolding.  
**Suitability:** Excellent match.  
A mini-binder could act as:

- A **molecular “patch”** restoring adhesion between mutant MPZ molecules.
- A stabilizer of surface expression by binding the extracellular domain.

This is another high-priority plasma membrane target, with well-mapped extracellular surfaces and structural data.

---

### **4. MFN2, GDAP1, SH3TC2, MTMR2, etc.**

These are **intracellular or organelle-associated proteins** (mitochondrial, cytosolic, endosomal).  
→ **Not suitable** for a membrane-optimized mini-binder platform, unless delivery is expanded to intracellular targets.

---

### **Strategic takeaway**

You have **three CMT subtypes that cleanly fit a plasma-membrane–targeting platform**:

|CMT Subtype|Gene|Localization|Potential Mini-binder Mechanism|Comment|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|CMT1A|PMP22|Plasma membrane (Schwann cells)|Normalize overexpression, modulate oligomerization|Strongest genetic prevalence|
|CMTX1|GJB1 (Connexin 32)|Plasma membrane gap junctions|Stabilize or restore channel function|High structural tractability|
|CMT1B|MPZ (Myelin Protein Zero)|Plasma membrane adhesion molecule|Rescue adhesion or folding|Excellent extracellular handle|

---

If your mini-binder library is optimized for **extracellular or transmembrane epitopes**, then PMP22, GJB1, and MPZ represent **the sweet spot of CMT biology**—accessible, genetically causal, and mechanistically close to the disease phenotype.
