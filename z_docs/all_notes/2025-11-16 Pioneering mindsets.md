Module 2: [[2025-11-16 What is pioneering]]


At Flagship, we embrace three pioneering mindsets that help us chart these uncharted territories: 
- [[#Future-back thinking]]: begins with a bold, detailed vision of what could be and works backward to make it real. 
- [[#Unreasonable]]: pushes us to pursue ideas that defy logic and stretch beyond what’s considered possible. 
- [[#Consumer of uncertainty]]: invites us to see uncertainty not as a threat but as fertile ground for discovery. Together, these mindsets enable us to imagine and build the future before it exists.

---
## 🚀 Future-back thinking
This mindset means starting with a bold, detailed vision of the future and working backward to define the steps to achieve it. This mindset rejects simple extrapolation from the present and instead uses the future as the anchor for innovation. 

This mindset requires not attempting to **“justify”** our inventions by comparing them to what exists today. Doing so would force us to describe the difference as incremental innovations. 

Instead, embracing this mindset forces ourselves and our audience to _shift the center of gravity_ to the **future solution**. When talking to external people or investors, for instance, we need to take them _immediately_ to the far-out destination, describe it to them first, and only then consider whether to contrast it with what exists today.

Here is an analogy. Imagine when the car was first invented and the existing solution was a horse-drawn carriage. You might be tempted to ask a car salesperson to explain the difference. They would explain that you don't have to feed a car, it won’t get cold or tired, etc. They would describe all the attributes that have nothing to do with the real value of a car since they are focused on the status quo. Instead, if they had resisted the urge to differentiate from what exists today and had thought about the vision of what a car could provide, it would completely alter the way they would describe the car.


> [!question] Question
> How would you describe the car? What's the future-back version of the car, when all you have is horse-drawn carriages?

## 🤪 Unreasonable
This mindset refers to the willingness to pursue ideas that cannot be reasoned or logically extrapolated from today’s knowledge. It requires **imagination**, **leaps of faith**, and **openness** to possibilities that may initially appear impossible or “science fiction.”

Our value hypotheses should be unreasonable. The gravitational pull of reasonableness prevents innovation. One way to determine if a hypothesis is unreasonable is to attempt to define its risk and benefit. Reasonable hypotheses allow measurement because they’re adjacent and can be viewed in a looking-forward-from-the-present mindset. Unreasonable hypotheses require a leap of faith, and a looking-backward-from-the-future mindset.

Here is an analogy. Imagine looking at the first airplane. You could imagine scaling up and thinking about the risks/rewards of increasing its capacity, wingspan, number of engines, etc. But it would take a leap of faith to look at that plane and envision a vehicle capable of going into space or breaking the sound barrier.


> [!question] Thought
> The unreasonable act of thinking that an "airplane" could get us to the far reaches of the universe is a good one. 
> 
> What would be other analogies? Medicines that can cure any disease by intervening at the wiring of the biological network!


## 🤔 Consumer of uncertainty
This mindset captures the belief that the **greatest opportunities are found in unprecedented spaces where outcomes cannot be predicted**. That is, amidst uncertainty, there are low-risk, high-value opportunities. This entails embracing the unknown, treating uncertainty as raw material to be transformed into manageable risk through exploration.

Most innovations people think of are in the adjacency zone, i.e., closely related to the latest existing product or solution. We can identify this adjacency zone by how far experts or key opinion leaders can comfortably project their knowledge and expertise.

It’s easier and safer to “innovate” in the adjacency zone, since people will be able to assess the likelihood of your success and feasibility of your idea (not necessarily correctly though). 

In contrast, **innovating outside the adjacency zone** (i.e., leaping), while often considered reckless, has many advantages: 
1) No competitors to worry about (at least initially); 
2) Flexibility to carve out and own the new innovative space; and 
3) By definition, the platform or solution being created will be innovative.
