**Computational Team Roadmap for Biotech Companies**

**Principles**

	* **Computational-first, not service**: Computation drives discovery strategy, not just downstream analysis.
	* **Generalist → Specialist progression**: Early hires wear many hats; scale brings depth and specialization.
	* **Master the data lifecycle**: From raw experiment design → data engineering → ML/AI modeling → biological/clinical insights.
	* **Tight wet–dry integration**: No silos—computational and experimental constantly feed each other.

⸻

**Org Structure**

The computational org should be structured into three technical pillars with a translational interface:

	1. **Data Engineering & Infrastructure** – pipelines, storage, integration, cloud.
	2. **Computational Biology & Bioinformatics** – multi-omics, network biology, structural biology.
	3. **AI/ML & Modeling** – predictive design, generative models, inference.
	4. **Translational Interface** – computational PMs and strategists ensuring outputs guide program decisions.

⸻

**Timeline of Growth**

**First Hire (Day 1)**

	* **Role**: Head of Computational Sciences (IC + leader).
	* **Mandate**: Define data strategy, build first pipelines, and deliver proof-of-value analyses that shape experimental choices.
	* **Profile**: A hybrid computational biologist with strong data science chops, or an ML scientist with deep biology intuition.

⸻

**Year 1 (~5–7 people)**

	* **Leadership**: First hire evolves into functional head.
	* **Team Composition**:
	* 1 Data Engineer (ETL, pipelines, cloud infra).
	* 1 DevOps / ML Ops engineer.
	* 1 Computational Biologist.
	* 1 Machine Learning Scientist.
	* 1 Data Scientist / Biostatistician.

**Objective**: Deliver an integrated end-to-end loop from raw data → model → interpretable results → experimental decisions.

⸻

**Year 5 (~25–40 people)**

The computational team becomes a **platform-level division**.

	* **Leadership**:
	* VP Computational Sciences.
	* Directors for Data Platform, Computational Biology, AI/ML.
	* Program-aligned computational leads embedded in each therapeutic/diagnostic program.
	* **Data Engineering (~8–10 people)**
	* Cloud engineers, database architect, ML Ops/compliance.
	* **Computational Biology (~10–12 people)**
	* Specialists in genomics, proteomics, structural biology, systems/network biology.
	* **AI/ML (~10–12 people)**
	* Deep learning researchers, computational chemists, benchmarking group.
	* **Translational Interface (~3–5 people)**
	* Computational PMs and strategists ensuring outputs feed portfolio decisions.

**Objective**: Generate computational-first molecules, biologics, or diagnostics; drive portfolio choices directly.

⸻

**Gating & Milestone-Driven Growth**

**First Hire → Year 1**

	* **Milestone**: Standards set, pipelines working, first analysis/model drives experimental decision.
	* **Gate**: Demonstrated that computation informs direction (not just retrospective analysis).

**Year 1 → Year 3**

	* **Milestone**: First full data loop (ingestion → model → experiment).
	* **Gate**: ROI validated—predictions influence targets/compounds/biomarkers. Infrastructure supports >2 programs.

**Year 3 → Year 5**

	* **Milestone**: Computational function matures into a **reusable platform** with validated predictions.
	* **Gate**:
	* Program decisions led by computational outputs.
	* Data volume/complexity justifies specialists.
	* Portfolio pull requires embedded computational leads.

Scaling is gated by **data richness**, **validated predictive value**, and **demand from portfolio**.

**Future-Backwards Perspective**

Most biotechs grow computation as a bolt-on service. The leverage comes from flipping this: **computation first, experiment as validation**. By year 5, the computational team should be at least as large as the wet lab, shaping scientific direction and portfolio outcomes.
