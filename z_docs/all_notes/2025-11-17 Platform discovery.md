Flagship is in the business of discovering breakthrough life sciences platforms and forming and building new companies that create value from those platforms.


# Session 1: Defining a Platform Technology and a Platform Company

Creating _transformative life science platforms_ lies at the heart of Flagship Pioneering’s strategy. Unlike many companies, Flagship does not seek value by exploiting adjacent opportunities in known technological or scientific spaces but instead focuses on **_exploring breakthrough opportunities in unprecedented terrain_**. And instead of trying to innovate specific products, Flagship focuses on **_innovating broad technological platforms_** that can generate a stream of future products. A key component of Flagship’s business model has been to start new companies around these novel platforms.

> [!tip] What is a platform company?
> A platform company is fundamentally different from the typical single-asset company one finds in much of the biotechnology industry. A platform company is built to generate multiple products from a foundational technological or scientific advance. This approach allows for repeated innovation and the leveraging technological capabilities across a range of markets.

Good platforms are not built on individual technologies, but instead are **_integrated systems of different technologies that can be leveraged as a repeatable engine of product discovery_**. Because platforms can generate multiple options for product development, platform-based business models are inherently **less risky** than traditional single-asset company models. 

A critical economic property of platforms is that **after the initial fixed investment costs of building the platform have been incurred**, product options can each be generated at relatively low marginal costs. In this regard, not all platforms are created equal. Some platforms enable the creation of new products more effectively and efficiently than others. A key differentiator is **“programmability”**—how easily a platform can be adapted across product opportunities with small changes.

For example, in life sciences, platforms based on nucleic acids offer inherent programmability: modifying a genetic sequence can produce entirely new molecules and products. Other platforms, such as those based on the microbiome, have proven to be far less programmable, as each new product opportunity requires more complex reconfigurations. Because programmability enables the efficient generation of more product opportunities, it creates more options (at lower cost) and thus reduces risks. In financial terms, **platforms based on programmable technologies have higher option value**.

The value of platforms ultimately depends on their capacity to generate product options repeatedly. A platform company is built on reproducibility—the ability to generate predictable and scalable solutions within a defined category.

However, platforms don’t immediately produce results. **_Platforms themselves take time to develop and validate_**. It can also take time to understand the applications for which the platform is best suited. And the _product concepts_ produced by platforms require **extensive investment** and years of further development and testing before they ever become commercial products.

This raises an important question: how do you measure value when building a platform? Unlike a single-asset company, where value is tied to a specific product’s success, **platform companies must articulate their value in terms of future potential**—something that is often uncertain and difficult to quantify.

The ability to define and measure value is critical for platform companies, particularly when engaging with investors and partners. Without clear milestones and a compelling articulation of potential impact, platforms struggle to gain a following.  

Ultimately, building a platform capability and a company that full exploits the value of that capability is a journey that begins with a “leap” into unknown scientific terrain, and evolves through an iterative process of experimentation, learning, and adaptation. 

### **Summary**
- A platform consists of a set of scientific principles, methods, tools, and technologies that, when harnessed together, create the potential to generate multiple product options.  
- Pioneering involves the discovery of platforms rooted in unprecedented scientific and technological terrain that open new value pools.
- Platforms value increases with the number of product options they can generate.  Programmability is an important property enabling optionality.
- Platforms evolve through an iterative process. Building a platform based on a “bold leap” requires tangible “proof points” along the way.


# Session 2: The Emergence of Platform Companies

Pioneering means being the first to create valuable ideas by directing scientific innovation and entrepreneurship at unknown spaces, far beyond the adjacencies that incumbents pursue.

Pioneering is, by definition, a highly uncertain undertaking. We start with questions that might never have been asked before (e.g. “what if….”). It is tempting to think that pioneering must be a “loose” or unstructured process, heavily dependent on random luck. Flagship’s institutionalized entrepreneuring philosophy could not be more different. We believe in the power of a disciplined approach to pioneering. 


Pioneering follows a four-phase process.
### **1) Explorations**

Flagship Labs poses farfetched hypotheses, exploring the question “What if...?” Our entrepreneurial scientists allow hypotheses to evolve through variation and selection, collaborating with external experts to test the weaknesses and strengths of new concepts. We create better and better hypotheses until we identify something that _might_ be a breakthrough, if only it worked. We call these imaginative inquiries "explorations,” and conduct 80 to 100 a year.

### **2) ProtoCos**
Promising explorations become prototype companies, or “ProtoCos,” and are assigned a number according to their place in the series of Flagship Labs ventures: “FL1,” “FL2,”… “FL63,” etc. Flagship Pioneering founding teams test concepts, and discontinue ProtoCos that cannot, for whatever reason, validate their science in the laboratory. We use the word "origination" to mean the conception, iteration, and launch of a new company around a distinct breakthrough. We originate eight to ten ProtoCos every year.

### **3) NewCos**
If the founding team can plausibly convert the question “What if…” to the answer, “It turns out...” a ProtoCo becomes a new company, or “NewCo,” with a name and significant capital commitment from Flagship. Each NewCo focuses on developing a proprietary platform that will deliver years of important new products, and recruits a board of directors, CEO, and leadership team. We form six to eight NewCos a year.

### **4) GrowthCos**

Ultimately, a NewCo is spun out to become a growth company, or “GrowthCo.” A GrowthCo’s leadership team attracts outside investors, forges partnerships, and builds a business that creates extraordinary future value, often operating as public companies. Twenty-five of Flagship Pioneering’s GrowthCos have completed IPOs since 2013.

While we describe the process in a simple linear way, this does not mean that we focus only on science early and commercial value later.  The process is one that should continuously integrate the advancement of the technology with the search for value in markets. We have learned from experience that from the earliest stages of the process, scientists working on the platform should engage with those who have insights about potential market applications and partnering opportunities.

Early interactions with potential partners can provide crucial insights that help shape a platform’s development. These discussions not only validate _scientific potential_ but also reveal _commercial opportunities_, guiding the evolution of an idea from an initial hypothesis to a viable business. One of the **greatest challenges** in building a platform company is determining **how much proof is enough before seeking partners** or proposing product concepts. Many early-stage ventures fall into the trap of over-refining a concept before confirming its viability. Instead of spending years perfecting an idea in isolation, **successful pioneers sequence their proof points strategically**, testing key assumptions in a way that balances speed with rigor.

At this stage, a few critical questions arise:

• Can the platform be **clearly described** in a way that others can understand?
• Can its **core components be demonstrated**, even if the full system isn’t yet operational?
• Does the platform have the **potential** to generate multiple valuable products?

A true platform is not just a breakthrough idea—it’s a **repeatable engine** capable of **consistently generating value**. The ability to clearly define a platform is a key indicator of its maturity. If a concept cannot be articulated with clarity, it is unlikely to function as a true platform.

Beyond clarity, a platform must also demonstrate **generalizability**. The more adaptable and scalable a platform is, the greater its long-term potential. If each new product requires extensive modifications, the platform loses its advantage. A strong platform operates like a well-designed system: flexible enough to create new opportunities, yet stable enough to maintain efficiency.

One of the defining features of a successful platform company is its **ability to adapt**. A rigid approach—one that ignores data, resists change, or clings to an initial plan despite new insights—can be fatal

Even the most adaptable platform must eventually prove itself to investors. The key to long-term success lies in the strategic sequencing of validation steps—demonstrating that the platform can consistently generate high-value outputs.

Validation is not just about scientific feasibility; it also involves business considerations such as manufacturing scalability, cost-effectiveness, and market fit. **A platform isn’t fully validated when a single product works**—it must show a repeatable ability to create multiple valuable products.

A pioneering platform company operates in a space of high uncertainty—where conviction must be balanced with adaptability. **Leaders must have the foresight to anticipate challenges and adjust their strategy without losing sight of the broader vision**.

This balancing act requires making strategic investments, forging the right partnerships, and ensuring that risk is distributed wisely. Overcommitting too early can be as dangerous as being too cautious—both extremes can stall progress.


### **Summary**

- Flagship uses a highly disciplined process for discovering novel platforms and creating value through the formation of entrepreneurial ventures. The process is based on a core principle of evolution in nature—better outcomes emerge through a highly iterative process of experimentation, learning, and adaptation.
- The process at Flagship integrates both scientific validity (will the platform work? What kind of technical solutions are needed to unlock the platform? Etc.) with economic validity (how and where will the platform create value?). This requires input along the way from various perspectives, including those of prospective partners and product developers. 
- Because of the pioneering nature of our platforms, the journey from concept to platform is not fraught with uncertainty.  Dealing with uncertainty requires adaptability and optionality.


# Session 3: Navigating the Unique Challenges of Building Platform Companies

Building a platform company presents a unique set of challenges. One of the **_biggest risks_** is spreading resources too thin—**attempting to develop multiple product concepts before the core platform is sufficiently validated**. At the same time, over-focusing on a single asset can limit the broader potential of the platform and prevent it from reaching its full scale.

One of the biggest challenges in building a platform company is striking the **right balance** between _focusing on core platform development_ and _pursuing multiple product opportunities_. Spread resources too thin, and the platform may never reach its full potential. Focus too much on a single product, and the broader vision may be lost. Successful platform leaders manage this tension by **carefully sequencing investments**. Rather than rushing to scale or placing all bets on one asset, they ensure the **platform is validated at each step** while strategically expanding into multiple applications.

Yet, even with a well-balanced approach, platform companies can fall into common strategic traps—mistaking difficulty for value, overcommitting resources prematurely, or waiting too long to make key decisions.

> [!tip] Avak Kahvejian
> - premature scaling: let's build everything in parallel because we want to be ready
>   - you may get lucky, but you may run out of money
>   - you're better off building in steps

Platform companies operate in a high-uncertainty environment, and the way they navigate decision-making and resource allocation can determine their success or failure.

A few common traps include:

1. **Fixating on the hardest problem** – Teams often assume that the most valuable opportunity is also the most difficult one. This can lead to an "all-or-nothing" approach that delays progress.
2. **Premature scaling** – Investing heavily across multiple areas before proving the platform’s viability can deplete resources too early.
3. **Failure to update thinking** – Leaders may become too attached to initial assumptions and miss critical opportunities to refine their approach.

Platform companies that fall into these traps risk stagnation or collapse. Instead, they must remain agile—constantly refining their approach, prioritizing feasible opportunities, and balancing ambition with pragmatism.

Even the most well-designed platform will face significant challenges—whether in resource allocation, regulatory hurdles, or market adoption. No company operates in isolation, and strategic partnerships can make the difference between success and failure. Flagship Pioneering has built an interconnected ecosystem where companies benefit not only from shared infrastructure and expertise but also from lessons learned across the entire ecosystem. This collective advantage helps mitigate risk, accelerate learning cycles, and improve the odds of success.

Yet, leveraging an ecosystem requires a delicate balance. **Companies must remain independent enough to drive their own vision while also tapping into the collective strength of the ecosystem**. Those that embrace this approach can increase both their probability of survival and their probability of success.

### **Summary**
- Platform validation is not a single event, but instead involves a step-by-step process of validating pieces and versions of the platform.  It is important to avoid an "all-or-nothing" mindset where the company aims to validate only the most advanced, future version of the platform.  
- Allocating resources between developing the core platform and pursuing multiple product candidates is a critical dilemma facing platform companies.  Balance is essential. The core platform requires investment, but showing the platform creates multiple viable platform opportunities is critical to demonstrating the functionality and utility of the platform.  The resource dilemma this imposes makes partnering and leveraging the Flagship ecosystem an essential feature of platform company strategies. 
- Avoid conflating difficulty with value. A hard problem to solve is not necessarily a valuable problem to solve.  
- Premature scaling and committing to development programs before the platform’s viability is understood leads to a high burn rate, which leaves the company very exposed. 
- In the process of building the platform, companies should be careful about distinguishing assumptions from facts.
