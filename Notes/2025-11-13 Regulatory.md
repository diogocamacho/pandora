**What an IND is (and why it exists)**

* The IND (Investigational New Drug application) is the legal mechanism that lets you ship and give a drug to humans across state lines before it’s approved. Purpose: ensure participants aren’t exposed to undue risk. You notify FDA, they review for safety; silence for 30 days = you may start.  

**When you do—and don’t—need an IND**

* **Always need IND** for any unapproved drug or “new drug” use under FDA’s broad definition: new molecular entity, new indication, new route, new dose/formulation, new population that increases risk.  
* **IND may be exempt** for studies using _approved_ U.S.-marketed drugs **within** labeling, or off-label only if **all** exemption criteria in 21 CFR 312.2(b) are met (no significant risk increase, noncommercial intent, IRB approval, informed consent, etc.). Cancer-specific exemption guidance exists; emergency/EFIC studies cannot claim exemption. When in doubt, call FDA first.  
* The **flowchart on page 13** distills the decision path: labeled use → no IND; unlabeled use → check exemptions; if not exempt, decide who sponsors and file Forms 1571, 1572, 3674; 30-day wait; address holds; then ongoing reporting.  

**Who’s who (and which Center)**

* **Sponsor-investigator** = the same person initiates _and_ conducts the study (common in academia/single-site). They are the IND holder and carry both sponsor and investigator obligations. 
* **Center jurisdiction:** most drugs (including many biologics) → **CDER**; blood, vaccines, tissues, cell/gene therapies → **CBER**. Contact points and pre-IND consults are available. 

**IND types you should be able to name**

* **Research/Investigator IND** (your default here).
* **Commercial IND** (corporate/NIH or with obvious commercialization intent).
* **Emergency IND** (single-patient emergency use).
* **Treatment IND** (serious/life-threatening access pre-approval).
* **Exploratory IND** (very early, microdosing/screening, no therapeutic intent). 

**What goes in a sponsor-investigator IND (lean version)**

* **Form 1571** (application): identify drug (trade/generic, dosage form/strength/lot); if using approved product **without** modifying packaging, CMC is minimal. Modifications or non-approved form require enough CMC/nonclinical/human data (or a right-of-reference letter). Include environmental categorical exclusion (21 CFR 25.31(e)) unless special cases. Initial serial number “0000.”
* **Form 1572** (Statement of Investigator): qualifications/site details (CV often attached).
* **Form 3674** ([ClinicalTrials.gov](http://ClinicalTrials.gov) certification): register “applicable clinical trials” (not Phase 1) and list the NCT number.
* Practicalities: triplicate submission; IND number assigned in FDA’s acknowledgment; project manager named; 30-day clock from receipt.  

**The 30-day clock, holds, and how to get moving**

* **Default**: if no FDA objection in 30 days → you may start. FDA usually won’t send an “approval” letter—::silence is consent::. 
* **Clinical hold** (partial or full): FDA lists issues; you must submit a **“Clinical Hold Complete Response”** (Form 1571 with correct serial/check box) addressing every point. FDA replies within ~30 days; do not start until the hold is lifted. 

**While the IND is active — the obligations you’re accountable for**

Think of this as your permanent checklist. It’s where sponsors get tripped up.

* **Protocol amendments (312.30):** New protocol, changes increasing/decreasing exposure, eligibility changes, safety monitoring changes, or adding investigators. IRB approval required. Prospective filing unless to remove an immediate hazard (then notify ASAP). 
* **Information amendments (312.31):** New tox/chemistry/technical info not otherwise reported; label clearly; don’t file more often than every 30 days.
* **Safety reporting (312.32):**
	* **Serious & unexpected** adverse experiences “associated with the drug”: written **IND Safety Report** to FDA, all investigators, IRB **within 15 calendar days**.
	* **Death or life-threatening**: phone/fax **within 7 calendar days**. You must assess significance in context of all reports. 
* **Annual report (312.33):** Due within **60 days of IND anniversary**: status of each protocol; enrollment by age/sex/race; completions/dropouts; interim results if any; summary of safety reports; significant new nonclinical/CMC info; plan for next year.
* **Drug accountability/monitoring (Subpart D duties):** You’re responsible for human subject protection, data integrity, protocol compliance, and **control of the investigational drug** (receipt, storage, dispensing, reconciliation). Fix problems or terminate and notify IRB/FDA.  

**End-states to keep straight**

* **Withdraw** anytime (account for drug; if safety-related, explain; notify IRB).
* **Termination**: FDA can terminate (usually with sponsor input; response window is short).
* **Inactive status**: e.g., delays, poor accrual, missing annuals; **≥5 years inactive** can lead to termination. 

**CFR map you should have in your head (Table on page 14)**

* **21 CFR 312** (the IND backbone): applicability (312.2), content/format (312.23), protocol & info amendments (312.30–31), safety (312.32), annuals (312.33), holds (312.42), termination/inactive (312.44–45), sponsor responsibilities (312.50), investigator duties (312.60–70).
* Adjacent regs you’ll cite: **21 CFR 50** (Informed Consent), **56** (IRBs), **201** (Labeling), **54** (Financial disclosure), **314** (NDAs), **316** (Orphan). 

**Talking points for regulators (and executives)**

* **Risk lens first.** FDA’s IND gate is safety-centric; tailor your package to ::demonstrate reasonable risk, not to prove efficacy::. Pull only the CMC/nonclinical detail necessary for your use case—more is not better if it’s irrelevant to the proposed exposure/population.
* **Sponsor-investigator ≠ “lighter weight” compliance.** The paperwork can be slimmer, but the accountability isn’t. Treat safety reporting timeliness and drug accountability as non-negotiable.
* **Plan for holds.** Pre-IND contact and crisp responses keep you out of “ping-pong.” Label your hold responses exactly as FDA expects and answer every bullet.
* **Register properly.** [ClinicalTrials.gov](http://ClinicalTrials.gov) certification (Form 3674) is now table-stakes for applicable studies; don’t let admin misses derail first-patient-in.  

**Memorize these “numbers & nouns”**

* **30 days**: Passive FDA review window before first dosing.
* **15 days / 7 days**: IND safety report timing (serious+unexpected; death/life-threatening).
* **60 days**: Annual report due after IND anniversary.
* **312.2(b)**: Exemption criteria; **312.23**: IND content; **312.30–33**: amendments/safety/annuals; **50/56**: consent/IRB.
* **Forms**: **1571** (IND), **1572** (Investigator), **3674** ([ClinicalTrials.gov](http://ClinicalTrials.gov)). 

**Forward-leaning posture (what sophisticated teams do)**

* **Design “exemption-ready” protocols** for labeled-use research when possible (keep risk unchanged, avoid commercial intent), but be ready to pivot to IND if any element nudges risk. 
* **Exploratory INDs** to derisk mechanism/PK with microdoses before you commit to full Phase 1—especially for platform portfolios that iterate quickly.
* **Pre-IND consults** (especially for complex modalities, antimicrobials/antivirals) to lock expectations and keep your 30-day review clean.
