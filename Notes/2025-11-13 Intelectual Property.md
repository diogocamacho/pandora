**Intelectual Property** 

_What a patent covers is defined by **the claims of the patent**. The claims of a patent are the sentences that you will find at the end of the patent whose language is negotiated with a patent office in order to define the metes and bounds of what the invention covers and what it does not. For example, when you have real property like a house or acreage or a farm, you can go down to your assessor's office and get a map of the property that will lay out the metes and bounds of that property. And that is the scope of your property, in that farm or that house or that property. _

_Something similar exists for patents: the role of the claims. The patent scope is defined by the claims. That is, the claims are what define the metes and bounds of what is covered by the patent. The rest of the patent, which might be hundreds of pages, is there to enable and support and describe the claims._



* What is a patent?
	* a limited monopoly to your invention
		* in exchange to disclosing the invention to the public (patent contract)
	* it’s control of usability, nothing else
	* Right to exclude others, and limited to the invention only
	* What is an invention?
		* new and useful process or improvement of a process
		* You need conception (intelectual act) and reduction to practice (embodiment of the concession)
			* reduction to practice comes in flavors:
				* taking invention, making it, describing how you made it (actual reduction to practice)
				* [constructive/prophetic] give instructions or a recipe on how to make your invention
					* allows us to file prophetic applications
					* Eg: early on in a protoco, we haven’t done it, but we can tell you how to do it
					* Once you have done it in a prophetic way, you can now show how it works in an actual reduction to practice



* Patent scope:

**Patentability requirements**

_The most critical requirements for getting a patent, patentability requirements, are: (1) Patentable subject matter; (2) Novelty; (3) Non-Obviousness; (4) Enablement; (5) Written Description; and (6) Definiteness. Whenever you're working with your IP business partner or me on putting together claims and understanding how we're going to claim our invention, we're going to walk through these requirements and make sure that we're meeting all of them._



	* **patentable subject matter**
		* composition of matter, processes, machines, articles of manufacture [technology] -> all patentable
		* **EXCLUDES**: Products of nature and natural processes
			* don’t over index one the fact that if we work on products of nature we cannot patent
	* **novelty**



**Timeline**

_When it comes to patents, it is important to be aware of the timeline and the journey of a patent from the time an application is filed to the time that the patent is granted. This often varies by country, so we'll consider the timeline in the United States. **Month zero** is the time of filing our first application. Ninety percent of the times, that's going to be what we call a **provisional application**. A provisional application is not itself published or examined. But, in a sense, it stops the clock with regard to what prior art can be used against you to show lack of novelty or lack of non-obviousness. _

_At 30 months, we're just beginning to go into the patent office, and then a patent office depending on the country might take anywhere from two to five years to grant you a patent. During those two to five years, we are negotiating with the patent officer in the form of office actions that come to us and responses that we make to the patent officers. We're negotiating on the scope of the claims. There will be rejections such as “Your invention is not novel, your invention is not enabled, your invention is not described.” And we make arguments in return, and ultimately, we convince the patent office of the appropriate scope of our claims, and we get a granted patent.  _

! 

_If you add all of this up, it can take several years to go from the original application to a granted patent. _

**Patent strategy in life sciences**

_There are a few considerations for parent strategy in the life sciences. There are different considerations in the tech sector._

_The final strategic consideration involves differences in approach between a technology (or modality) company and a discovery or biology company. _

_A technology company develops a novel therapeutic modality for therapeutics of agriculture, such as mRNA, where the core features of future products are apparent from the outset. Because the platform itself can be clearly described and claimed early, this strategy lends itself to broad foundational patents filed at an early stage. _

_In such cases, filing early is valuable because the modality underpins future drug products, and strong foundational patents can secure wide-ranging protection across the pipeline._



* how early to file and what to file? 
	* the earlier you file on platform, the earlier they become prior art for your platform
	* once it becomes prior art, there will be obvious rejections
	* early but not so early -> a healthy tension
	* what is the competitive landscape?
* what kind of platform is this? 
	* is this a discovery platform? -> platform IP is less relevant to prior art for the product
	* we only know the right answer in restropect
	* early stage investors see value in patents, but potential partners in dev may see the value in delaying
* policeability and enforceability
	* patents on platforms make it hard to see who may be infringing on our patents
	* patents on regulated products like drugs it is easier to see who may be infringing
* tech vs discovery companies
	* we file patents on tech platforms early (moderna, sail)
	* when the platform is a discovery engine, we will be agnostic for the final modality. those platforms are difficult to police, so we file for limited number of patents, to ensure we protect the innovation space for those platforms.

**Exclusivity and freedom to operate**

_Exclusivity and freedom to operate are related but distinct concepts in patent strategy. **Exclusivity** comes from the patents you own: it is the legal right to prevent others from making, using, or selling your invention. **Freedom to operate (FTO),** on the other hand, is about ensuring that you can develop and commercialize your product without infringing on patents owned by others. In practice, a company may have strong exclusivity through its own patents but still lack FTO if other parties hold blocking patents on essential technologies needed to bring the product to market._



* exclusivity: what do our patents look like
* FTO: other people’s patents
