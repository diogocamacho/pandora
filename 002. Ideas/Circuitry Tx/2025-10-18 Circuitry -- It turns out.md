## 🧠 Thought/note

The “It turns out…” for Circuitry:

### 1. It turns out that transcriptomic states are mathematically controllable.
- Liu et al., Cell Systems 2023 showed that applying control-theory metrics to GRNs identifies minimal driver nodes capable of switching cell fates (e.g., fibroblast ↔ myofibroblast).
- This means disease states can, in principle, be “steered” back to health using finite, computable interventions — precisely what Circuitry exploits.


### 2. It turns out synthetic RNAs can execute multi-input logic in living cells.
- Green & Collins, Nature 2014; Chen et al., Science 2021 demonstrated RNA toehold switches and dual-function RNAs that perform AND/NOT operations.
- These devices validate that RNA molecules can implement Boolean-like control at the molecular level — a precedent for RNA-based “logic gates.”


### 3. It turns out multi-target RNA modulation yields emergent, synergistic correction of network errors.
- Recent CRISPRi/siRNA multiplex screens (Nat Biotech 2023) show that simultaneously modulating small gene sets can restore pathological transcriptional profiles better than single-target interventions.
- Circuitry’s multi-activity RNAs embody this principle natively.


### 4. It turns out large perturb-seq atlases enable predictive modeling of cell-state transitions.
- Regev et al., Science 2022 built cell-by-gene-by-perturbation matrices covering millions of measurements; machine learning can now forecast expression shifts from arbitrary perturbations.
- That’s the training corpus for the Circuitry Engine™ — the RNA compiler that learns how to translate “disease → healthy” network transitions.
