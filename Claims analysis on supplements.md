> [!faq] **What are you trying to do?**
> This prompt attempts to do a comprehensive market analysis on claims of supplement products, on long term company branding and messaging, on customer perceived benefit. 
>  




## Prompt
```

**Role & Expertise**  
You are a senior market strategist focused on dietary supplements, functional foods, and performance/hydration drinks.  
You have deep working knowledge of:

- Market trends and segment dynamics in supplements and functional beverages.
    
- Consumer psychology in health/wellness purchasing.
    
- Acquisition economics (CAC, LTV, payback, retention drivers).
    
- How language in claims and positioning influences click-through, trial, and long-term brand affinity.
    
- The regulatory landscape around structure/function claims (e.g., FDA/FTC in the U.S.) and how brands “dance on the line” without making explicit disease claims.
    

**Objective**  
Analyze and compare the claims and positioning strategies used by the following supplement / drink / functional nutrition brands and products:

- Elysium (e.g., Basis and related NAD+ products)
    
- Nutrafol
    
- Gatorade
    
- BodyArmor
    
- Nature Valley
    
- Liquid I.V.
    
- Seed
    
- AG1 (Athletic Greens)
    
- Ritual
    
- Muscle Milk
    
- Celsius
    
- Fairlife
    
- Niagen / ChromaDex
    
- Nutricia
    
- Vitaflo
    
- DripDrop
    

Focus on how these companies use **claims and language** to:

- Differentiate in a crowded market.
    
- Justify premium pricing.
    
- Lower perceived risk and build trust.
    
- Drive repeat purchase and long-term relationships.
    

**Methodology & Data Use**

- Use web browsing to review:
    
    - Official websites and product pages.
        
    - Taglines, front-of-pack language, landing page copy, FAQs.
        
    - Where available: ads, influencer copy, or meta-descriptions summarizing claims.
        
- Do **not** fabricate claims. Only describe claims and language that are supported by what you find.
    
- You may quote short phrases (e.g., taglines or short claim snippets) but keep them brief and comment on them rather than reproducing long blocks of copy.
    
- If something is unclear or conflicting across sources, call it out explicitly instead of glossing over it.
    

**For Each Brand / Product (one section per brand)**  
Provide a concise but substantive breakdown with the following structure:

1. **Positioning & Target Use Case(s)**
    
    - What category are they in (e.g., “longevity/NAD+,” “hair health,” “electrolyte hydration,” “daily greens,” “infant medical nutrition,” etc.)?
        
    - Who is the primary target customer (e.g., “biohacker/professional,” “female 25–45 wellness-seeker,” “performance athlete,” “busy parent,” “clinical/medical use”)?
        
2. **Core Claims & Promises**
    
    - Summarize the main types of claims they make. Examples:
        
        - Performance (energy, stamina, recovery).
            
        - Aesthetic (hair, skin, body composition).
            
        - Health maintenance / optimization (gut health, immune support, longevity).
            
        - Disease-adjacent or quasi-clinical (e.g., cognitive decline, metabolic health, hydration in illness).
            
    - Note whether these are framed as:
        
        - “Supports X”, “helps maintain Y” (structure/function).
            
        - Stronger, more outcome-oriented language that might push against regulatory boundaries.
            
3. **Language & Psychology**
    
    - Analyze the tone and vocabulary. Examples:
        
        - Scientific / clinical vs lifestyle / aspirational vs performance-sport.
            
        - Use of “clean,” “evidence-based,” “doctor-formulated,” “NSF-certified,” “clinical-grade,” etc.
            
        - How they frame time horizon: quick wins vs long-term health vs preventative.
            
    - Explain how their language likely interacts with:
        
        - **Trust** (credentials, science, social proof).
            
        - **Desire** (status, performance, aesthetics, energy, longevity).
            
        - **Risk reduction** (safety, transparency, ingredient sourcing).
            
4. **Customer Relationship & Retention Design**
    
    - How do they set up for a long-term relationship? For example:
        
        - Subscriptions, habit-building cues, “daily ritual” framing.
            
        - Education (blogs, podcasts, deep FAQs, science sections).
            
        - Community, coaching, or check-ins.
            
        - Guarantees, trials, or easy cancellation policies.
            
    - Comment on how their claims and promises align with **long-term use** vs one-off trial.
        
5. **CAC & Funnel Implications (Qualitative)**
    
    - Infer their likely acquisition strategy and CAC drivers from the positioning:
        
        - Heavy paid social vs search vs retail shelf vs clinical channel vs influencer.
            
        - Whether their claims support **direct-response performance marketing** or rely more on brand building and trust.
            
    - Do **not** invent precise CAC numbers unless you find credible reported ranges. Instead, qualitatively discuss whether their positioning suggests:
        
        - High CAC but high LTV (deep trust, subscription).
            
        - Lower CAC via broad awareness and commodity positioning.
            
6. **Regulatory & Credibility Posture**
    
    - How close do their claims get to explicit disease treatment?
        
    - How heavily do they lean on:
        
        - Clinical trials, peer-reviewed evidence, or patents.
            
        - Certifications (NSF, Informed Sport, etc.).
            
    - Comment on whether their language feels conservative, balanced, or aggressive from a regulatory/credibility perspective.
        

**Cross-Brand Synthesis (after the individual sections)**  
Provide an integrated analysis across all brands:

1. **Claim Archetypes**
    
    - Identify 4–6 recurring “claim archetypes” you see across these brands. Examples:
        
        - “Science-backed longevity”.
            
        - “Clean, traceable, transparent wellness”.
            
        - “Hydration as performance medicine”.
            
        - “Everyday better energy & focus”.
            
        - “Food as functional medicine / medical nutrition”.
            
    - Map which brands sit in which archetypes and where there is white space.
        
2. **Language Patterns that Seem to Work**
    
    - Highlight recurring words, phrases, and structures that likely increase conversion and long-term trust (e.g., “ritual,” “daily foundation,” “clinically studied,” “backed by X trials,” “no artificial Y”).
        
    - Call out which patterns are likely **fads** vs **durably effective** based on broader consumer trends.
        
3. **Psychological Levers**
    
    - Compare how brands balance:
        
        - Fear vs aspiration.
            
        - Quick wins vs long-term prevention.
            
        - Science vs “natural/clean/feel-good”.
            
    - Explain how these differences likely affect:
        
        - Upfront conversion.
            
        - Repurchase and subscription adherence.
            
4. **Long-Term Brand Relationship Design**
    
    - Compare which brands are best set up for high LTV relationships and why.
        
    - Note tactics such as: merchandise, content ecosystems, apps/trackers, clinical partnerships, or personalization.
        
5. **Implications for CAC & LTV**
    
    - For each major archetype, discuss:
        
        - How the claim and language strategy likely impacts CAC.
            
        - How it impacts retention/LTV.
            
        - Typical trade-offs (e.g., “heavier education → higher CAC but better selection and retention”).
            

**Output Format**

- Use clear headings: one section per brand, then a final “Cross-Brand Synthesis”.
    
- Where useful, include a **summary comparison table** with rows as brands and columns such as:
    
    - Primary category / use case
        
    - Target consumer
        
    - Dominant claim archetype
        
    - Tone (clinical / lifestyle / sports)
        
    - Relationship model (subscription vs one-off / hybrid)
        
    - Qualitative CAC/LTV posture (e.g., “high-CAC/high-LTV, education-heavy”).
        
- Keep the analysis tightly focused on **claims, language, and customer psychology**, not just general company descriptions.
    
- Be explicit about what is directly observed from sources vs what is your interpretation or inference.

```
